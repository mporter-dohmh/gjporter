NOT ONLY NEEDLES

DISCUSSION AT NEEDLE EXCHANGE SITES ABOUT HIV RELATED SERVICES
Judith Porter, PhD, Dept of Sociology,  Bryn Mawr College, Bryn Mawr, PA USA
Gilbert Perez, Community College of Philadelphia, Philadelphia. PA USA

BACKGROUND
HIV testing and use of drug treatment, medical care, and social services help prevent HIV among injection drug users.
Needle exchange programs are an important source of information about these services.
We investigate the effect of race and gender on communications about service acquisition between site personnel and exchangers.

METHODS - CLIENTS
Proportional random sample of exchangers by site (N=202) from four largest sites of Prevention Point Philadelphia needle exchange program in 2000.
DEMOGRAPHICS
White 51%
African American 39%
Latino 10%
Male 76%
Female 24%
Median Age-44 (Range 19-81)

METHODS - STAFF
Site personnel available for interaction with clients includes staff, volunteers, medical students, research assistants, and staff from other agencies.
Average of 8 personnel per site, range from  4-13 depending on site size.
Site personnel diverse by race and gender.

METHODS: RESEARCH
Questionnaires were orally administered to exchangers at the sites asking them:
What services were discussed.
With whom they were discussed.
Whether they had utilized HIV testing (available at all sites) and medical care (available at 3 sites).

RESULTS: SERVICE DISCUSSION
48% of exchangers discussed at least one service with site staff.
75% of discussions initiated by site personnel.
.

DISCUSSION OF SERVICES OFFERED ON SITE VS. ACTUAL SERVICE USE
Use of on-site medical services less frequent than discussion about medical care (for three sites where medical care available).
Use of HIV testing less frequent than HIV test discussions.

EXCHANGER INITIATED SERVICE DISCUSSIONS WITH SITE PERSONNEL BY RACE
Latinos less likely than other groups to initiate discussion on all services except drug treatment, where they were most likely to initiate discussion.
Latinos more likely to have questions they did not ask site personnel.
African Americans were most likely to initiate discussions on service issues.

EXCHANGER INITIATED SERVICE DISCUSSIONS BY GENDER AND SERVICE TYPE

RACE CONSONANCE IN SERVICE DISCUSSIONS
African American exchangers are more likely to initiate service discussions with African American personnel, especially about drug treatment.
African American exchangers are more likely to be offered drug treatment help by personnel of their own race (p=.02).
Whites and Latinos did not interact significantly more frequently with personnel of their own race.

EXCHANGER INITITATED DISCUSSION WITH SAME RACE PERSONNEL

GENDER CONSONANCE IN SERVICE DISCUSSIONS
African American males are more likely to discuss drug treatment services with African American male personnel.
There is no gender consonance in service discussions for any other racial group.

MALE EXCHANGER DISCUSSION WITH MALE PERSONNEL: DRUG TREATMENT

CONCLUSIONS
Sites that serve African Americans should have African American personnel, especially African American males.
Site personnel should routinely initiate discussions about a range of services and elicit questions from males and Latinos.
There was no gender consonance in initiation of service discussions by female exchangers.

CONCLUSIONS
Findings should be replicated in other programs, especially those serving Latinos, since the Latino sample in this study was small.
